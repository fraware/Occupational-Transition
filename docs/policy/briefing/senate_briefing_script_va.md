# 2-minute spoken script (Virginia senator brief)

Senator, this briefing uses reproducible public federal data to answer a narrow
question: what can we credibly say about AI and labor conditions in Virginia
today, and what should federal policy do next.

First, Virginia's labor structure in our retained benchmark frame is
concentrated in health care and social assistance and manufacturing. In 2025
Q3, those shares are about 37.6% and 28.7%, respectively, within the six-sector
benchmark denominator.

Second, Virginia's profile is meaningfully different from nearby peers. In the
retained state set, Virginia ranks first in retail share and fourth in
manufacturing share. That means national averages alone can misstate where
monitoring and adjustment pressure is likely to appear in Virginia.

Third, we now have a practical signal stack for oversight: structural
composition and wage context, peer comparisons, rank diagnostics, and a compact
Virginia KPI dashboard. This is sufficient for transparent monitoring, but not
for causal attribution.

The near-term federal strategy is straightforward.

In the first 0 to 3 months, finalize CPS AI worker module wording, lock BTOS AI
core item continuity, and enforce publication metadata standards.

In 3 to 9 months, field and publish stable CPS and BTOS outputs in recurring
dashboard form.

In 9 to 18 months, launch a scoped JOLTS supplement pilot to improve
occupation-aware demand context without attempting immediate full redesign.

A final guardrail: this brief is descriptive, not causal. It does not estimate
worker-firm linked treatment effects, and it does not support fine-grained
local monthly causal claims. But it is strong enough to guide oversight,
appropriations questions, and staged federal measurement upgrades now.
