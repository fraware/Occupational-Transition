# Figure 6 — Survey-design roadmap from the public-data identification frontier

**What is shown.** The figure connects the paper’s current public observables to its decisive blind spot, then maps that diagnosis to a sequential survey-design agenda. The left block summarizes the margins the present public stack already observes: worker-side CPS signals, business-side BTOS adoption evidence, and structural/contextual layers from OEWS, O*NET, JOLTS, and CES. The middle block isolates the missing integrated object: no core public source jointly links worker occupation, employer AI adoption, and subsequent worker outcomes. The right block shows the paper’s recommended marginal interventions and the longer-run linked-administrative horizon.

**How to read it.** Read from left to right. The figure does not argue that current public data are empty; it argues that they are modular and informative up to a visible frontier. The recommended interventions are therefore not generic “more data” proposals. They are targeted responses to the exact places where the public architecture now runs out.

**Interpretation.** The figure makes the paper’s policy logic explicit: preserve the strongest current series, add a short worker-side AI-at-work module in CPS, maintain BTOS continuity while using ABS as the annual structural business complement, and test a narrowly scoped JOLTS pilot for coarse occupation-aware demand context. These steps improve observability without pretending to solve the missing worker-firm AI linkage problem.

**Boundary.** This is a design and synthesis figure, not a statistical estimate. It formalizes the paper’s survey-design agenda but does not itself provide causal identification.
